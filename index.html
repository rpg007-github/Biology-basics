The Chemistry of Life

Elements of Life

The six most abundant elements in living organisms: C, H, N, O, P, S (CHNOPS). These account for ~98% of the mass of most organisms.

Most abundant element in the human body by mass: oxygen (~65%). Most abundant by atom count: hydrogen.

Water: The Molecule of Life

Water (H₂O) is a polar molecule: oxygen pulls electrons closer to itself, giving it a slight negative charge (δ−), while each hydrogen carries a slight positive charge (δ+). This polarity is what makes water special and gives rise to nearly all of its biologically important properties.

  • Universal solvent: water's polarity lets it dissolve more substances than any other common liquid. Polar and ionic substances dissolve easily (hydrophilic); nonpolar substances do not (hydrophobic).
  • Hydrogen bonding: the δ+ hydrogen of one water molecule is attracted to the δ− oxygen of a neighbor. Each water molecule can form up to 4 hydrogen bonds. These bonds are individually weak but collectively account for all of water's unusual physical properties.
  • Cohesion: water molecules stick to each other (hydrogen bonds). This creates surface tension, which is why insects can walk on water and why water forms droplets.
  • Adhesion: water molecules stick to other polar surfaces (like glass or the walls of xylem vessels in plants). Cohesion + adhesion together drive capillary action, helping water travel upward through plant stems.
  • High specific heat (4.18 J/g·°C): water absorbs a lot of heat before its temperature rises. This stabilizes body temperature and moderates Earth's climate. It takes a lot of energy to heat water up, and water releases that energy slowly as it cools.
  • High heat of vaporization: it takes a lot of energy to evaporate water. This is why sweating and transpiration cool organisms so effectively: the most energetic water molecules leave as steam, carrying heat away.
  • Ice floats: when water freezes, hydrogen bonds lock molecules into an open lattice, making ice less dense than liquid water. This is why lakes freeze from the top down, insulating aquatic life below. Almost no other substance does this.
  • pH of pure water = 7.0 (neutral)
  • Human body is ~60% water by mass

pH Scale

  • Scale runs 0 to 14
  • <7 = acidic, 7 = neutral, >7 = basic
  • Each unit = 10-fold change in [H⁺]
  • pH = −log[H⁺]
  • Blood pH: 7.35 to 7.45
  • Stomach pH: ~1.5 to 3.5

  • Van der Waals (weakest): transient dipoles

Weak bonds are individually fragile but collectively powerful (e.g., DNA base pairing, protein folding).

The Cell

Cell Theory

  • All living things are made of cells
  • The cell is the basic unit of life
  • All cells come from pre-existing cells

Key Organelles & Functions

  • Nucleus: houses DNA, site of transcription
  • Ribosome: protein synthesis (translation)
  • Rough ER: protein processing, has ribosomes
  • Smooth ER: lipid synthesis, detoxification
  • Golgi apparatus: modify, sort, ship proteins
  • Mitochondria: ATP production (cellular respiration)
  • Chloroplast: photosynthesis (plants/algae)
  • Lysosome: intracellular digestion
  • Vacuole: storage (large central vacuole in plants)
  • Cytoskeleton: structure, movement, division

Plant vs. Animal Cells

Plant cells have, animal cells lack: cell wall (cellulose), chloroplasts, large central vacuole, plasmodesmata.

Animal cells have, plant cells lack: centrioles, lysosomes (typically).

Both have: nucleus, mitochondria, ER, Golgi, ribosomes, plasma membrane.

Endosymbiotic Theory

Mitochondria and chloroplasts were once free-living prokaryotes engulfed by ancestral eukaryotic cells. Evidence: they have their own circular DNA, double membranes, 70S ribosomes, and divide by binary fission.

Membranes & Transport

Membrane Structure

  • Fluid mosaic model: phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins
  • Phospholipids are amphipathic: hydrophilic heads, hydrophobic tails
  • Cholesterol modulates fluidity (in animal cells)
  • Integral proteins span the membrane; peripheral proteins sit on the surface

Transport Types

TypeEnergy?Direction
Simple diffusionNo (passive)High → Low
Facilitated diffusionNo (passive)High → Low, via protein
OsmosisNo (passive)Water: low solute → high solute
Active transportYes (ATP)Low → High
Endocytosis/ExocytosisYes (ATP)Bulk transport via vesicles

Tonicity

  • Hypertonic: cell loses water, shrivels (crenation in animal cells, plasmolysis in plant cells)
  • Hypotonic: cell gains water, swells (lysis in animal cells, turgid in plant cells)
  • Isotonic: no net water movement

Water always moves toward higher solute concentration.

Metabolism & Energetics

ATP: The Energy Currency

  • Adenosine triphosphate
  • Energy released by hydrolysis of terminal phosphate bond
  • ATP → ADP + Pᵢ + energy
  • ATP is recycled, not stored long-term

Enzymes

  • Biological catalysts (mostly proteins)
  • Lower activation energy; do NOT change ΔG
  • Substrate binds at active site
  • Induced fit model (not rigid lock-and-key)
  • Affected by: temperature, pH, substrate concentration, inhibitors
  • Competitive inhibitor: binds active site
  • Noncompetitive (allosteric) inhibitor: binds elsewhere, changes shape

Thermodynamics

  • 1st Law: Energy is conserved (not created or destroyed)
  • 2nd Law: Every energy transfer increases entropy
  • Exergonic (ΔG < 0): spontaneous, releases energy (e.g., catabolism)
  • Endergonic (ΔG > 0): non-spontaneous, requires energy input (e.g., anabolism)

DNA, RNA & the Genetic Code

DNA Structure: The Big Picture

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is the molecule that stores the instructions for building and running every living thing. It lives in the nucleus of eukaryotic cells, packaged into chromosomes. The structure of DNA directly explains how it works: it stores information, copies itself, and passes instructions to the next generation.

  • Shape: a double helix, like a twisted ladder. Two strands wind around each other.
  • Backbone: the "rails" of the ladder are made of alternating sugar (deoxyribose) and phosphate groups, connected by phosphodiester bonds. This backbone is the same throughout the molecule.
  • Bases (the rungs): the "rungs" of the ladder are pairs of nitrogenous bases. There are four: adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G), cytosine (C). The sequence of these bases IS the genetic information, just like the sequence of letters spells out words.
  • Base pairing rules: A always pairs with T (held by 2 hydrogen bonds), and G always pairs with C (held by 3 hydrogen bonds). G-C pairs are stronger because they have more hydrogen bonds. This is called complementary base pairing.
  • Chargaff's rules: because of complementary pairing, in any DNA molecule: %A = %T and %G = %C. If you know 30% of bases are adenine, then 30% are thymine, and the remaining 40% is split evenly between G and C (20% each).
  • Antiparallel strands: the two strands run in opposite directions. One runs 5' to 3', the other 3' to 5'. This matters for replication and transcription because enzymes can only work in the 5' to 3' direction.
  • The double helix is ~2 nm wide. One full turn of the helix spans about 10 base pairs.

Nucleotide: The Building Block

Each nucleotide (the monomer of DNA and RNA) has three parts:

  • A 5-carbon sugar (deoxyribose in DNA, ribose in RNA)
  • A phosphate group
  • A nitrogenous base (A, T, G, or C in DNA; A, U, G, or C in RNA)

Purines (two rings): A and G. Pyrimidines (one ring): C, T, and U. A purine always pairs with a pyrimidine, which keeps the helix width constant.

Think of DNA as the master blueprint that stays safely in the nucleus, and RNA as the working copy that carries instructions out to the ribosomes.

Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

This is the fundamental flow of genetic information in cells:

DNA → (transcription) → mRNA → (translation) → Protein

  • Replication: DNA copies itself (DNA → DNA). Happens during S phase of the cell cycle.
  • Transcription: a gene's DNA sequence is copied into messenger RNA (mRNA). This happens in the nucleus. RNA polymerase reads the template strand and builds mRNA in the 5' to 3' direction.
  • Translation: the ribosome reads the mRNA and builds a protein, one amino acid at a time. This happens at ribosomes in the cytoplasm. Transfer RNA (tRNA) brings the correct amino acid to match each 3-letter codon on the mRNA.

The Genetic Code

  • 64 codons total (4 bases × 4 × 4 = 4³)
  • These 64 codons code for 20 amino acids plus stop signals
  • Degenerate (redundant): most amino acids have more than one codon (e.g., leucine has 6 codons). This is a built-in buffer against mutations.
  • Universal: nearly all organisms on Earth use the same genetic code, from bacteria to humans. This is strong evidence for common ancestry.
  • Start codon: AUG (codes for methionine, also signals "begin reading here")
  • Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA (do not code for any amino acid)
  • Read in triplets (3 bases = 1 codon = 1 amino acid), no gaps, no overlap

DNA Replication Key Facts

  • Semiconservative: each new DNA molecule has one old strand and one new strand (proven by Meselson-Stahl experiment)
  • New DNA is always built in the 5' to 3' direction
  • Leading strand: synthesized continuously toward the replication fork
  • Lagging strand: synthesized in short pieces (Okazaki fragments) away from the fork, then joined together
  • Key enzymes: Helicase unwinds the double helix, Primase lays down a short RNA primer, DNA polymerase adds new nucleotides and proofreads, Ligase seals the gaps between fragments

Types of Mutations

  • Point mutations (single base changes): substitution, insertion, deletion
  • Silent: no change in amino acid (thanks to code degeneracy)
  • Missense: wrong amino acid inserted
  • Nonsense: creates a premature stop codon
  • Frameshift: insertion or deletion shifts the entire reading frame, usually devastating

Gene Expression Control

  • Prokaryotic: operons (lac operon, trp operon)
  • Eukaryotic mRNA processing: 5' cap, poly-A tail, splicing (remove introns, keep exons)
  • Regulation at: transcription, RNA processing, translation, post-translational
  • Remember: "Exons are expressed, introns are in the way"

Cell Division

The Cell Cycle

  • Interphase (~90% of cycle): G1 → S → G2
  • G1: growth, normal cell functions
  • S phase: DNA synthesis (replication)
  • G2: preparation for division
  • M phase: mitosis + cytokinesis
  • G0: non-dividing state (e.g., neurons)

Mitosis (PMAT)

  • Prophase: chromosomes condense, spindle forms, nuclear envelope breaks down
  • Metaphase: chromosomes align at metaphase plate
  • Anaphase: sister chromatids separate, move to poles
  • Telophase: nuclear envelopes reform, chromosomes decondense

Result: 2 identical diploid (2n) daughter cells

Meiosis

  • Two divisions: meiosis I (homologs separate) and meiosis II (sister chromatids separate)
  • Result: 4 haploid (n) daughter cells
  • Produces gametes (sperm, eggs)
  • Sources of genetic variation: crossing over (prophase I), independent assortment (metaphase I), random fertilization

Mitosis vs. Meiosis at a Glance

FeatureMitosisMeiosis
Divisions12
Daughter cells2 (diploid)4 (haploid)
GeneticallyIdenticalUnique
Crossing overNo (rare)Yes
PurposeGrowth, repairGamete production

Chromosome Numbers

  • Humans: 2n = 46 (23 pairs); n = 23
  • 22 pairs of autosomes + 1 pair of sex chromosomes
  • Female: XX; Male: XY
  • Diploid (2n): two copies of each chromosome
  • Haploid (n): one copy of each chromosome

Mendelian & Population Genetics

Mendel's Laws

  • Law of Segregation: two alleles separate during gamete formation
  • Law of Independent Assortment: genes on different chromosomes sort independently
  • Law of Dominance: dominant allele masks recessive in heterozygote

Key Genetic Terms

  • Genotype: genetic makeup (e.g., Bb)
  • Phenotype: observable trait (e.g., brown eyes)
  • Homozygous: BB or bb
  • Heterozygous: Bb
  • Dominant: expressed in heterozygote
  • Recessive: expressed only when homozygous

Classic Crosses

  • Monohybrid (Bb × Bb): phenotype ratio 3:1, genotype ratio 1:2:1
  • Dihybrid (BbCc × BbCc): phenotype ratio 9:3:3:1
  • Testcross: unknown × homozygous recessive to reveal genotype

Beyond Simple Dominance

  • Incomplete dominance: blending (red × white = pink)
  • Codominance: both expressed (AB blood type)
  • Multiple alleles: e.g., ABO blood types (Iᴬ, Iᴮ, i)
  • Polygenic: multiple genes for one trait (skin color, height)
  • Epistasis: one gene masks another
  • Pleiotropy: one gene affects multiple traits
  • Sex-linked: genes on X chromosome (e.g., color blindness, hemophilia)

Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

Allele frequencies remain constant if: no mutation, no selection, random mating, no gene flow, large population.

p + q = 1 (allele frequencies)

p² + 2pq + q² = 1 (genotype frequencies)

where p = dominant allele frequency, q = recessive allele frequency

Evolution & Taxonomy

Natural Selection (Darwin)

Four conditions: variation exists in a population, variation is heritable, there is a struggle for existence (overproduction), and individuals with favorable traits have differential reproductive success (fitness).

Types of Selection

  • Directional: favors one extreme
  • Stabilizing: favors the average (e.g., birth weight)
  • Disruptive: favors both extremes
  • Sexual selection: traits favored for mating success

Mechanisms of Evolution (Beyond Selection)

  • Genetic drift: random changes in allele frequency (bottleneck effect, founder effect)
  • Gene flow: movement of alleles between populations
  • Mutation: ultimate source of new alleles
  • Nonrandom mating

Evidence for Evolution

  • Fossil record
  • Homologous structures (same origin, different function)
  • Analogous structures (different origin, similar function = convergent evolution)
  • Vestigial structures (reduced, nonfunctional remnants)
  • Embryology (similar developmental stages)
  • Molecular biology (DNA/protein sequence comparisons)
  • Biogeography

Speciation

  • Allopatric: geographic barrier separates populations
  • Sympatric: speciation without geographic separation (e.g., polyploidy)
  • Reproductive isolation: prezygotic (before fertilization) and postzygotic (after fertilization) barriers

Taxonomic Classification

Domain → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

"Dear King Philip Came Over For Good Spaghetti"

Three Domains: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya

Six Kingdoms: Bacteria, Archaea, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, Animalia

Binomial nomenclature: Genus species (italicized, genus capitalized). Example: Homo sapiens

Ecology & Biogeochemistry

Levels of Ecological Organization

Organism → Population → Community → Ecosystem → Biome → Biosphere

A population is all individuals of one species in an area. A community is all the different species in that area. An ecosystem includes the community plus the nonliving environment (water, soil, sunlight, temperature).

Energy Flow & the 10% Rule

Energy flows through an ecosystem in one direction only (it is NOT recycled like nutrients). At each step of the food chain, only about 10% of energy passes to the next level. The rest is lost as heat through metabolism. This is why food chains are short and why there are far fewer top predators than plants.

  • Producers (plants, algae) make their own food via photosynthesis
  • Primary consumers (herbivores) eat producers
  • Secondary consumers (carnivores) eat herbivores
  • Tertiary consumers (top predators) eat other carnivores
  • Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) break down dead matter at every level, recycling nutrients back into the soil

Example: If grass captures 10,000 kcal of solar energy, a rabbit eating that grass gets ~1,000 kcal, and a hawk eating the rabbit gets ~100 kcal.

Biogeochemical Cycles

Unlike energy, matter is recycled through ecosystems. The same carbon, nitrogen, and water atoms cycle through living and nonliving systems over and over.

  • Water cycle: evaporation, condensation, precipitation, transpiration, runoff
  • Carbon cycle: photosynthesis pulls CO₂ out of air; respiration and combustion put it back
  • Nitrogen cycle: nitrogen fixation (bacteria convert N₂ gas into usable NH₃), nitrification, denitrification
  • Phosphorus cycle: no gaseous phase; moves from rock to soil to organisms and back

Population Ecology

  • Exponential growth (J-curve): population grows without limits, gets faster and faster. Happens when resources are unlimited.
  • Logistic growth (S-curve): population grows, then levels off at the carrying capacity (K), which is the maximum number the environment can support long-term.
  • r-selected species: many offspring, little parental care, short lives (insects, bacteria, weeds)
  • K-selected species: few offspring, lots of parental care, long lives (elephants, whales, humans)
  • Density-dependent factors: things that get worse as population grows (competition, disease, predation)
  • Density-independent factors: affect populations regardless of size (hurricanes, fires, droughts)

Species Interactions

These describe how two species affect each other when they interact. The + means the species benefits, − means it is harmed, and 0 means it is unaffected.

Ecological Succession

Ecological succession is the process by which the species in an ecosystem change over time, gradually building up complexity. Think of it like a vacant lot slowly becoming a forest over decades.

  • Primary succession: starts from nothing (bare rock, no soil). Pioneer species like lichens and mosses arrive first, slowly break down rock into soil, and make it possible for grasses, shrubs, and eventually trees to grow. This takes hundreds to thousands of years. Example: new volcanic island.
  • Secondary succession: starts from disturbance where soil already exists (e.g., after a forest fire or abandoned farmland). Because soil is already present with seeds and roots, recovery is much faster, usually decades.
  • Both types progress toward a stable climax community (like a mature forest) that persists until the next major disturbance.

Human Anatomy & Physiology Essentials

Organ Systems Overview

  • Integumentary: skin, hair, nails; protection, thermoregulation
  • Skeletal: 206 bones; support, protection, movement, blood cell production
  • Muscular: ~600 skeletal muscles; movement, heat production
  • Nervous: brain, spinal cord, nerves; rapid signaling
  • Endocrine: hormones; slow, long-lasting chemical signaling
  • Cardiovascular: heart, blood vessels; transport O₂, nutrients, waste
  • Lymphatic/Immune: defense, fluid return
  • Respiratory: gas exchange (O₂ in, CO₂ out)
  • Digestive: nutrient breakdown and absorption
  • Urinary: kidneys filter blood, regulate fluid/electrolytes
  • Reproductive: production of gametes and offspring

Blood & Circulation

  • Blood types: A, B, AB, O (+ Rh factor)
  • Universal donor: O−
  • Universal recipient: AB+
  • Heart flow: body → vena cava → R atrium → R ventricle → pulmonary artery → lungs → pulmonary vein → L atrium → L ventricle → aorta → body
  • Normal resting heart rate: ~60 to 100 bpm
  • Normal blood pressure: ~120/80 mmHg

Nervous System Basics

  • CNS: brain + spinal cord
  • PNS: everything else (somatic + autonomic)
  • Autonomic: sympathetic ("fight or flight") + parasympathetic ("rest and digest")
  • Neuron: dendrites → cell body → axon → synaptic terminals
  • Resting potential: ~−70 mV
  • Action potential: all-or-none depolarization via Na⁺ influx, then K⁺ efflux
  • Signal crosses synapse via neurotransmitters

Immune System

  • Innate immunity: nonspecific, immediate (skin, phagocytes, inflammation, fever)
  • Adaptive immunity: specific, slower, has memory
  • B cells: produce antibodies (humoral immunity)
  • T cells: cell-mediated immunity (helper T, cytotoxic T)
  • Antibodies: Y-shaped proteins; bind specific antigens
  • Vaccination: stimulates memory cells without causing disease

Key Hormones

  • Insulin (pancreas): lowers blood glucose
  • Glucagon (pancreas): raises blood glucose
  • Epinephrine (adrenal medulla): fight-or-flight
  • Thyroid hormones (T3/T4): metabolism rate
  • ADH (posterior pituitary): water reabsorption in kidneys
  • Estrogen/Testosterone: sex hormones
  • Growth hormone (anterior pituitary): growth, cell reproduction

Essential Formulas

Cellular Respiration & Photosynthesis

Respiration:

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP

Photosynthesis:

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

These are essentially the reverse of each other.

Hardy-Weinberg

p + q = 1

p² + 2pq + q² = 1

p² = frequency of AA; 2pq = frequency of Aa; q² = frequency of aa

pH

pH = −log[H⁺]

pOH = −log[OH⁻]

pH + pOH = 14

Population Growth

Exponential: dN/dt = rN

Logistic: dN/dt = rN(K − N)/K

where N = population size, r = intrinsic rate of increase, K = carrying capacity

Free Energy

ΔG = ΔH − TΔS

ΔG < 0: exergonic (spontaneous); ΔG > 0: endergonic (non-spontaneous)

Temperature Conversion

°C = (°F − 32) × 5/9

°F = (°C × 9/5) + 32

K = °C + 273.15

Magnification

Total magnification = ocular lens × objective lens

Typical ocular = 10×. Objectives: 4×, 10×, 40×, 100× (oil immersion).

The Scientific Method

  • Observation → Question → Hypothesis → Experiment → Analysis → Conclusion
  • Independent variable: what you change
  • Dependent variable: what you measure
  • Control group: no treatment, baseline comparison
  • A good hypothesis is testable and falsifiable
  • Theory: well-supported, broadly explanatory (not "just a guess")
