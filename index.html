Biology 1 Basics

The essential ideas, explained simply, with examples that make them stick.

This guide covers the core concepts of introductory biology in plain language. Each topic is explained first, then reinforced with examples and memory tricks, so you understand the "why" before you memorize the "what."

1

The Chemistry of Life

The Elements That Make You

Living things are built from just a handful of elements. Six of them make up about 98% of your body mass: Carbon, Hydrogen, Nitrogen, Oxygen, Phosphorus, and Sulfur.

Memory trick: "CHNOPS" (say it like "schnops"). These six are the building blocks of every protein, every strand of DNA, and every molecule of fat in your body.

Why Water Is Special

Water (H₂O) is a polar molecule, meaning one end is slightly positive and the other is slightly negative. Think of it like a tiny magnet. This polarity is the reason water can do so many things that other liquids cannot.

Because water molecules are polar, they stick to each other through hydrogen bonds. These bonds are individually weak, but because there are so many of them, they give water remarkable properties:

It dissolves almost everything (polar and ionic substances), which is why your blood can carry nutrients and your cells can run chemical reactions.

It resists temperature changes (high specific heat), which is why your body temperature stays stable even when it's hot or cold outside.

Ice floats because frozen water is less dense than liquid water. This insulates lakes in winter and keeps aquatic life alive underneath.

Think of It This Way

Imagine water molecules as tiny magnets. The positive end of one is attracted to the negative end of its neighbor. This attraction (hydrogen bonding) is why water beads up on a penny (cohesion), climbs up a paper towel (adhesion + capillary action), and takes a long time to boil (high heat of vaporization).

Key numbers: pH of pure water = 7.0 (neutral). pH of blood = 7.35 to 7.45. Your body is about 60% water.

The pH Scale

The pH scale measures how acidic or basic a solution is. It runs from 0 (extremely acidic) to 14 (extremely basic), with 7 being neutral. The critical thing to understand is that each step on the scale represents a 10-fold change in acidity.

Worked Example

How much more acidic is a solution at pH 3 compared to pH 5?

Each step = 10x more H⁺ ions. Two steps = 10 x 10 = 100 times more acidic.

The Four Macromolecules

All living things are built from four categories of large molecules. Each is a polymer (long chain) built from smaller monomers (building blocks), snapped together like beads on a string.

CarbohydrateMonosaccharide (simple sugar)GlycosidicStarch, cellulose

Building polymers removes water (dehydration synthesis). Breaking them apart adds water back in (hydrolysis). Think: "dehydration" = "remove water."

Chemical Bonds, Ranked by Strength

Not all bonds are equal. Here they are from strongest to weakest:

Covalent (strongest): atoms share electrons. These hold molecules together.

Ionic: one atom gives an electron to another. Think table salt (NaCl).

Hydrogen bonds: weak attractions between polar molecules. Individually weak, but collectively powerful. They hold DNA strands together and give water its properties.

Van der Waals (weakest): temporary, flickering attractions. They help geckos stick to walls.

A good analogy: covalent bonds are like bolts holding a bridge together. Hydrogen bonds are like sticky notes. One sticky note peels off easily, but a thousand of them can hold a heavy book to a wall.

The Cell

Cell Theory (Three Big Ideas)

1. All living things are made of cells.

2. The cell is the basic unit of life (the smallest thing that is "alive").

3. All cells come from pre-existing cells (life doesn't spring from nothing).

Prokaryotes vs. Eukaryotes

This is the most fundamental divide in cell biology. The key difference: eukaryotes have a nucleus (a membrane-wrapped compartment holding DNA), and prokaryotes do not.

FeatureProkaryoteEukaryote
Nucleus?No (DNA floats in the cell)Yes (DNA in nucleus)
SizeSmall (1 to 10 µm)Larger (10 to 100 µm)
DNA shapeCircularLinear chromosomes
Organelles?None with membranesMany (mitochondria, ER, etc.)
ExamplesBacteria, ArchaeaAnimals, Plants, Fungi
Think of It This Way

A prokaryotic cell is like a studio apartment: everything happens in one open room. A eukaryotic cell is like a house with separate rooms (organelles) for different tasks: a home office (nucleus), a kitchen (mitochondria for energy), a mailroom (Golgi apparatus), etc.

Key Organelles and What They Do

You don't need to memorize every organelle right away, but these are the ones that come up constantly:

Nucleus: the "control center." Stores the cell's DNA and is where genes are read (transcription).

Ribosomes: tiny protein-building factories. They read mRNA instructions and assemble proteins.

Mitochondria: the "powerhouse." Burns glucose to produce ATP (the cell's energy currency).

Chloroplast (plants only): captures sunlight and converts it into sugar (photosynthesis).

Cell membrane: the flexible boundary that controls what enters and leaves the cell.

Golgi apparatus: the "post office." Packages and ships proteins to their destinations.

Plant cells have three things animal cells don't: a rigid cell wall (made of cellulose), chloroplasts (for photosynthesis), and a large central vacuole (for water storage). Both cell types have mitochondria, ribosomes, and a cell membrane.

Endosymbiotic Theory

This explains where mitochondria and chloroplasts came from. The idea: billions of years ago, a large cell swallowed a smaller prokaryotic cell but didn't digest it. Instead, they formed a partnership. The small cell became the mitochondrion (or chloroplast), living inside the larger cell and providing it with energy.

Evidence that this actually happened: mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own circular DNA (like bacteria), their own ribosomes (bacterial-sized, 70S), double membranes (the inner one was the original bacterium's membrane), and they divide by binary fission (the way bacteria reproduce).

Membranes & Transport

The Cell Membrane

The cell membrane is a phospholipid bilayer: two layers of phospholipid molecules arranged tail-to-tail. Each phospholipid has a water-loving (hydrophilic) head and two water-fearing (hydrophobic) tails. The heads face the watery environment on both sides, and the tails hide in the middle.

Proteins are embedded in this bilayer like icebergs floating in a sea. This is called the fluid mosaic model: "fluid" because the pieces move around, "mosaic" because it's made of many different parts.

How Things Get In and Out

The cell needs to let some things in (nutrients, water) and keep other things out (toxins). There are two main categories of transport:

Passive transport (no energy needed): molecules move from where there are MORE of them to where there are FEWER, like a ball rolling downhill.

Active transport (requires ATP energy): molecules are pushed from where there are fewer to where there are more, like pushing a ball uphill.

TypeNeeds Energy?Direction
Simple diffusionNoHigh concentration to low
Facilitated diffusionNoHigh to low, through a protein channel
OsmosisNoWater moves toward higher solute concentration
Active transportYes (ATP)Low concentration to high (against gradient)

Osmosis and Tonicity

Osmosis is just the diffusion of water across a membrane. Water moves toward wherever the solute (dissolved stuff) is more concentrated.

The effect on a cell depends on the solution it's sitting in:

Hypotonic solution (dilute, less solute outside): water rushes IN. The cell swells. Animal cells can burst (lyse). Plant cells become firm (turgid), which is good for them.

Hypertonic solution (concentrated, more solute outside): water rushes OUT. The cell shrivels. Animal cells wrinkle (crenation). Plant cells pull away from their wall (plasmolysis).

Isotonic: equal concentration inside and out. No net water movement.

Worked Example

A red blood cell is placed in distilled water (pure water, no solute). What happens?

Distilled water is hypotonic (less solute outside than inside the cell). Water moves IN by osmosis. The red blood cell swells and bursts (lyses).

"Hypo Hippo": a cell in a hypotonic solution swells up like a hippo. "Hyper Shrivels": a cell in a hypertonic solution shrivels up.

Energy & Metabolism

ATP: The Energy Currency

Cells don't burn food directly. Instead, they break food down and transfer the energy into a small molecule called ATP (adenosine triphosphate). When a cell needs energy for anything, it snaps the last phosphate off ATP, releasing energy. Think of ATP as a rechargeable battery: it gets charged up during cellular respiration and spent whenever the cell does work.

ATP → ADP + Pᵢ + energy

Enzymes: Biological Speed-Ups

Enzymes are proteins that speed up chemical reactions by lowering the activation energy (the energy barrier that must be overcome to start a reaction). They don't change whether the reaction happens or how much energy it releases; they just make it happen faster.

Each enzyme has a specific active site where the substrate (the molecule being acted on) fits. The shape has to match, which is why enzymes are specific: lactase breaks down lactose but nothing else.

Think of It This Way

Imagine you need to roll a boulder over a hill to get it to the other side. The hill is the activation energy. An enzyme is like someone who digs a tunnel through the hill. The boulder still ends up on the other side, but it takes much less effort to get there.

Enzymes are affected by temperature and pH. Too hot or too extreme a pH and the enzyme "denatures" (its shape unfolds and it stops working). This is why high fevers are dangerous.

Cellular Respiration: Turning Food Into Energy

This is how your cells extract energy from glucose. The overall equation:

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ~30-32 ATP

In plain English: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water + energy. This happens in three main stages:

1. Glycolysis (in the cytoplasm): splits glucose in half. Produces 2 ATP. Does not need oxygen.

2. Krebs Cycle (in the mitochondrial matrix): processes the fragments from glycolysis. Produces 2 ATP and a lot of electron carriers (NADH, FADH₂).

3. Electron Transport Chain (inner mitochondrial membrane): the big payoff. Uses those electron carriers to produce about 26-28 ATP.

If no oxygen is available, the cell can only do glycolysis, followed by fermentation to recycle its supplies. This produces only 2 ATP per glucose. In your muscles, fermentation makes lactic acid. In yeast, it makes alcohol and CO₂ (which is how beer and bread are made).

Photosynthesis: Turning Sunlight Into Food

This is essentially cellular respiration in reverse. Plants, algae, and some bacteria capture light energy and use it to build glucose from CO₂ and water:

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

It happens in two stages inside the chloroplast:

Light reactions (thylakoid membranes): capture sunlight, split water, produce ATP and NADPH, release O₂.

Calvin cycle (stroma): uses the ATP and NADPH to build sugar from CO₂.

Chlorophyll absorbs red and blue light and reflects green, which is why plants look green.

DNA, RNA & the Genetic Code

DNA Structure

DNA is shaped like a twisted ladder (double helix). The sides of the ladder are a sugar-phosphate backbone. The rungs are pairs of nitrogenous bases. There are four bases:

Adenine (A) pairs with Thymine (T) (held by 2 hydrogen bonds)

Guanine (G) pairs with Cytosine (C) (held by 3 hydrogen bonds)

The sequence of these bases IS the genetic information, just like the sequence of letters spells out a book.

"Apples in Trees (A-T), Cars in Garages (C-G)"
Worked Example — Chargaff's Rules

If a DNA sample is 20% adenine, what are the other percentages?

A always pairs with T, so T = 20%. That accounts for 40% total. The remaining 60% is split evenly between G and C: G = 30%, C = 30%.

DNA vs. RNA

Think of DNA as the master blueprint that stays safe in the nucleus. RNA is the working copy that carries instructions out to the ribosomes where proteins are built.

FeatureDNARNA
SugarDeoxyriboseRibose
StrandsDoubleSingle
BasesA, T, G, CA, U, G, C (uracil replaces thymine)
LocationNucleusNucleus and cytoplasm

The Central Dogma: DNA → RNA → Protein

This is the fundamental flow of genetic information. It has two main steps:

Step 1: Transcription (in the nucleus). The cell copies a gene's DNA sequence into a messenger RNA (mRNA) molecule. Think: "transcription" = "rewriting in a new format," like transcribing a lecture into notes.

Step 2: Translation (at ribosomes in the cytoplasm). The ribosome reads the mRNA three letters at a time (each group of three is called a codon). Each codon specifies one amino acid. Transfer RNA (tRNA) brings the correct amino acid, and the ribosome links them together into a protein. Think: "translation" = "converting one language (nucleotides) into another (amino acids)."

Key Facts About the Genetic Code

There are 64 possible codons (4 bases x 4 x 4) coding for just 20 amino acids plus stop signals. This means most amino acids have more than one codon (the code is "redundant"), which provides a buffer against mutations.

Start codon: AUG (also codes for methionine). Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA.

The genetic code is nearly universal: bacteria, plants, and humans all use the same code. This is powerful evidence for common ancestry.

DNA Replication

Before a cell divides, it must copy its DNA so each daughter cell gets a complete set. The process is semiconservative: each new DNA molecule has one original strand and one brand-new strand.

Key enzymes: Helicase unzips the double helix. DNA polymerase builds the new strand (always in the 5' to 3' direction). Ligase glues fragments together.

Mutations

A mutation is any change in the DNA sequence. Some matter, some don't:

Silent: the base changes but the amino acid stays the same (thanks to the redundant code). No effect.

Missense: one wrong amino acid is inserted. May or may not cause problems.

Nonsense: creates a premature stop codon. The protein is cut short. Usually bad.

Frameshift: an insertion or deletion shifts every codon downstream. Almost always devastating, like removing a letter from a sentence and re-reading it:

Frameshift Analogy

Original: THE BIG RED FOX RAN FAR

Delete "B": THE IGR EDF OXR ANF AR (everything after the deletion is garbled)

Cell Division

Mitosis: Making Identical Copies

Mitosis is how your body grows and repairs itself. One cell divides into two identical daughter cells, each with the full set of chromosomes (diploid, 2n = 46 in humans). The phases spell PMAT:

Prophase: chromosomes condense and become visible; nuclear envelope breaks down.

Metaphase: chromosomes line up in the Middle of the cell.

Anaphase: sister chromatids are pulled Apart to opposite poles.

Telophase: Two new nuclei form; chromosomes decondense.

"Please Make Another Taco" (Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase)

Meiosis: Making Sex Cells

Meiosis produces gametes (sperm and egg cells). It involves two rounds of division, resulting in 4 genetically unique haploid cells (n = 23 in humans).

What makes meiosis special is that it shuffles the genetic deck. Three mechanisms create variation:

Crossing over (prophase I): homologous chromosomes swap segments of DNA.

Independent assortment (metaphase I): each pair of chromosomes lines up randomly.

Random fertilization: any sperm can meet any egg.

Genetics

Mendel's Laws (The Basics)

Law of Segregation: every organism has two copies of each gene (one from mom, one from dad). When it makes gametes, the copies separate so each gamete gets only one.

Law of Independent Assortment: genes on different chromosomes are inherited independently of each other.

Law of Dominance: if you have one dominant allele and one recessive allele, the dominant one determines your appearance.

Key Vocabulary

Genotype: the actual alleles you carry (e.g., Bb). Phenotype: what you look like (e.g., brown eyes).

Homozygous: two of the same allele (BB or bb). Heterozygous: two different alleles (Bb).

Classic Crosses

A Punnett square is a simple grid that shows all possible offspring from a cross.

Worked Example — Monohybrid Cross

Cross two heterozygous parents: Bb x Bb

The Punnett square gives: BB, Bb, Bb, bb

Genotype ratio: 1 BB : 2 Bb : 1 bb

Phenotype ratio: 3 dominant : 1 recessive (3:1)

Worked Example — Dihybrid Cross

Cross: BbCc x BbCc (tracking two traits at once)

Expected phenotype ratio: 9:3:3:1

Beyond Simple Dominance

Incomplete dominance: blending. A red flower crossed with a white flower produces pink offspring.

Codominance: both alleles are fully expressed. In AB blood type, both A and B antigens appear on the red blood cells.

Sex-linked traits: genes on the X chromosome (like color blindness, hemophilia). Males (XY) are more affected because they have only one X.

Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

This is a mathematical model that predicts allele frequencies in a population that is NOT evolving. If the population is in equilibrium (no evolution happening), then:

p + q = 1 (allele frequencies)

p² + 2pq + q² = 1 (genotype frequencies)

Where p = frequency of the dominant allele and q = frequency of the recessive allele.

Worked Example

In a population, 16% are homozygous recessive (aa). Find the carrier frequency.

q² = 0.16, so q = 0.4. Then p = 1 - 0.4 = 0.6.

Carriers (heterozygotes) = 2pq = 2(0.6)(0.4) = 0.48 = 48%

Five conditions for Hardy-Weinberg: no mutation, no selection, random mating, no gene flow, large population. If any of these are violated, the population is evolving.

Evolution

Natural Selection: The Four Requirements

Natural selection is the mechanism by which populations adapt over time. It requires four conditions:

1. Variation: individuals in the population differ in their traits.

2. Heritability: those differences are passed from parents to offspring.

3. Overproduction: more offspring are born than the environment can support.

4. Differential reproductive success: individuals with traits better suited to the environment survive and reproduce more.

Classic Example

Darwin's finches: on different Galápagos islands, finches with beak shapes suited to the local food source (large seeds, insects, etc.) survived and reproduced more than those with less-suited beaks. Over many generations, this led to different species of finch on different islands.

Evidence for Evolution

Fossil record: shows change over time.

Homologous structures: same origin, different function (your arm, a whale's flipper, a bat's wing all have the same bones).

DNA comparisons: organisms that look similar also have similar DNA sequences. Humans and chimps share about 98% of their DNA.

Vestigial structures: reduced, nonfunctional remnants (your appendix, a whale's tiny leg bones).

Taxonomic Classification

All living things are organized into a nested hierarchy:

Domain → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

"Dear King Philip Came Over For Good Spaghetti"

Three Domains: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya. Species are named with two-part Latin names: Homo sapiens (genus capitalized, species lowercase, italicized).

Ecology

Energy Flow and the 10% Rule

Energy flows through an ecosystem in one direction (it is not recycled). At each step of the food chain, only about 10% of energy passes to the next level. The other 90% is lost as heat through metabolism.

Worked Example

If grass captures 10,000 kcal of solar energy:

A rabbit eating that grass gets: 10,000 x 10% = 1,000 kcal

A hawk eating the rabbit gets: 1,000 x 10% = 100 kcal

This is why food chains are short (usually 4-5 levels max) and why there are far more plants than top predators.

Species Interactions

When two species interact, each one is either helped (+), harmed (-), or unaffected (0):

InteractionEffectExample

Population Growth

Exponential growth (J-curve): when resources are unlimited, a population grows faster and faster without limit.

Logistic growth (S-curve): in the real world, populations hit a ceiling called the carrying capacity (K), the maximum number the environment can support long-term. Growth slows and levels off.

Think of It This Way

Imagine bacteria in a fresh petri dish of food. At first they double every hour (exponential). But eventually the food runs low and waste builds up. Growth slows and the population plateaus at K (logistic).

Ecological Succession

Primary succession: starts from bare rock (no soil). Pioneer species like lichens arrive first and slowly break down rock into soil. Takes centuries. Example: a new volcanic island.

Secondary succession: starts after a disturbance where soil already exists (forest fire, abandoned farm). Much faster, usually decades.

Both progress toward a stable climax community.

Human Body Essentials

Blood and the Heart

The heart is a four-chambered pump. Blood flows in a loop:

Body → Vena cava → R atrium → R ventricle → Lungs → L atrium → L ventricle → Aorta → Body

The right side pumps blood to the lungs (to pick up oxygen). The left side pumps oxygenated blood to the entire body.

Universal donor: O- (no antigens). Universal recipient: AB+ (no antibodies against A, B, or Rh).

Normal resting heart rate: 60-100 bpm. Normal blood pressure: about 120/80 mmHg.

The Nervous System

Central nervous system (CNS): brain + spinal cord.

Peripheral nervous system (PNS): all the nerves going to and from the rest of the body.

The autonomic (automatic) nervous system has two branches: sympathetic ("fight or flight," speeds things up) and parasympathetic ("rest and digest," slows things down).

A neuron sends signals electrically along its length, then chemically across the gap (synapse) to the next cell using neurotransmitters.

Neuron resting potential: about -70 mV. Normal body temperature: 37°C (98.6°F).

Hormones to Know

Insulin: lowers blood sugar (from pancreas).

Glucagon: raises blood sugar (from pancreas). Think of insulin and glucagon as a seesaw keeping blood sugar balanced.

Epinephrine (adrenaline): fight-or-flight response.

ADH: tells kidneys to reabsorb water.

Lab & Scientific Method

The Scientific Method

Science follows a cycle: Observation → Question → Hypothesis → Experiment → Analysis → Conclusion.

Independent variable: the one thing you deliberately change.

Dependent variable: the thing you measure (it "depends" on your change).

Control group: gets no treatment, so you have a baseline to compare against.

Worked Example

Experiment: Does fertilizer affect plant growth?

Independent variable: amount of fertilizer (what you change).

Dependent variable: plant height (what you measure).

Control group: plants that get no fertilizer.

A hypothesis must be testable and falsifiable. "Plants grow taller with fertilizer" is testable. "Plants have feelings" is not (currently). Also: correlation does not equal causation.
